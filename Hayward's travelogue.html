    Arrival at Quebec, and train journey Quebec to Vancouver

    Arrival at Quebec about 8.30 p.m.
    Ships time. Monday night.
    The sight almost beggared description -
    land on each side, whilst the
    mountains on each side were numerous,
    The land looks like waste, with green
    dotted here and there.

    The day was really glorious, scarcely a
    ripple on the sea; whilst just after sea
    the sunset was lovely, particularly
    the effects behind the mountains.

    On deck the effect was beautiful -
    lights twinkling all round the
    harbour, whilst these was not a
    ripple on the sea; in fact the effect
    was even better than the exhibition.

    Swarms of telegraph lads
    came about at night, taking
    down messages for relatives etc.
    when we finally retired after enjoying
    [Page 2]

    the view, we were all too excited
    to sleep.

    2.

    May 1912 Tuesday

    Up at 4.0 a.m., and up on deck
    at 4.30 a.m., watching unloading
    of cargo, and dodging more and
    more telegraph messages.

    Breakfasted at 5.0 a.m., then all
    waited impatiently orders to go
    ashore.

    About 6.30 a.m; we landed and
    straightway were ushered to-
    Immigration Offices (adjoining
    the quay) where we had our
    Rail-Orders examined. We then
    had to leave our portable
    luggage in a large room,
    and were herded for Doctors
    Examination.
    This consisted of going down a
    long passage (men hats off) and [Page 3]


    the examination consisted of a
    nod to focus on.

    After being questioned as to means,
    we then renewed our mail address,
    and was then instructed to go to
    Luggage Dept, to claim same, have
    it examined by Customs, then to get
    ticketed by fly, after which it
    would be forwarded.

    As we were on the way to the
    shed, we were claimed by representative of
    various religious ⟨bodies⟩ and introduction
    tickets handed to us by church of
    England clergymen whilst we also
    received packs etc.

    On reaching the luggage shed,
    We soon found my box, but
    not the cases, which after patient
    searching we found far away.
    I soon found a Customs official,
    who asked a question or two, [Page 4]


    and then passed my boxes (I
    might incidentally mentions that I
    ripped him 8% at first). After a
    slight delay, a small lift arrived
    up a railway officer, who labelled
    my boxes, so then we returned
    to our portable luggage.

    We waited for an hour, and
    then got on the train, (after making
    Sunday Journalists) and prepared
    for our journey, but, to our
    dismay, we had a 3 hours wait
    'eve stacking.

    A 2% lift got about 1 llNote: unit of cheese,
    1 llNote: unit of butter, and 6 small boxes
    from the steward, whilst we bought
    two loanes here (5 cents each) 2 pieces
    of cowned beef (small) a bottle of
    milk (10 cents) and other [?]dices
    A young fellow from Radford (who
    knows Mr. Attenllorough) is with us. [Page 5]


    and as the train is crowded,
    we have arranged to sleep on
    the seats (which pull out and meet)
    and leave our goods in the
    hammock as I should say —
    sleeping bed, which is of wood.
    no mattress or anything.

    We are now moving along
    nicely,Note: there is a word covered by a black line，the scenery is lovely
    and the day beautiful.

    Fields abound on every side, with
    horses dotted here and there,
    and there is a range of mountains
    all along one side, which makes
    a shrinking picture; in fact I keep
    looking as I write, and it is
    most lovely.

    There is a Canadian opposite us
    who has given us many useful
    hints, a man from Nottingham
    behind, and a mother and child;

    4.

    Sunday

    Just had breakfast + have stopped
    half way between Calgary and
    Vancouver
    Was stopped here 2 hours at 2-0 a.m.
    shunting to so have been viewing
    the scenery since.

    It is wonderful and needs the
    pencil of an artist to describe,
    and even then he could not do
    it full justice.

    We have been running through
    these vast mountains, with great
    peaks flanking on other side,
    snow covered, and look perfect
    when down in the valley these [Page 25]


    are miniature wakes:
    falls and streams, and the
    colour is a beautiful sea:
    blue and it is one constant charm to watch.

    Right from the main lines
    to the top of the mountain
    are huge trees, very much
    like his knees, ranging from 10 to 40 feet in height.

    Have passed through the
    Great Loop (picture 5,053)
    of views 9 am sending,
    but the view does not

    The rail looks very

    We have just passed one

    whole journey. A river
    about as wide as the
    [?]ent, with lonely green
    ferns + trees beyond, whilst
    in the back-ground is
    these large mountains, with
    the peak of the centre one

    Again the scenery is —
    changed, and we are on
    the top of a large valley,
    down on our left is voiced
    coloured foliage, with little
    huts and streams mingled,
    whilst to the night are huge
    trees, with varying coloured

    mountain frowning down;
    On the left hand a descent [Page 27]


    bay equal to the Park shops,

    Some of the mountains,
    seem to taper into the
    very heavens above, and
    with the peaks snow covered
    the effect is grand, it shows
    nature at the next, and
    one feels the insignificance
    of human beings, against
    the works of the almighty.

    On the mountain sides
    are many crevices, and
    the heal of the sun melting
    the snow, causes the wakes
    to rickle down the vast
    heights, into the green valleys
    below.

    There is a nice view here, and

    loving time running alongside
